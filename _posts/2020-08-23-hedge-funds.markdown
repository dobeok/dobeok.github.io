Hedge fund strategies can be classified as **directional** or **non-directional**

---

Non-directional strategies have low market exposure, often labeled as relative value funds, they include:

- Convertible arbitrage
- Fixed income arbitrage
- Equity Market Neutral

Directional strategies have higher market exposure and include:

- Equity Long/Short
- Macro
- CTA/Managed Futures

Event-driven funds may be directional or non-directional. They include:

- Merger arbitrage or risk arbitrage
- Distressed/Special Situation

---

### Non-directional strategies

#### Convertible arbitrage

- A convertible arbitrage strategy aims to profit from mispricing opportunities within convertible bonds.
- Convertible securities are a combination of various instruments, and the parcel as a whole may have a different price than the sum of component parts.
- If the price is different, there is an opportunity to buy (sell) the parcel and sell (buy) the component parts to lock in a profit.
- A typical investment is to buy the convertible bond and sell short the common stock of the same company to take advantage of the stock's price volatility.

#### Fixed income arbitrage

- Managers aim to profit from price anomalies between related interest rate securities.
- Most managers trade globally, with a goal of generating steady returns with low volatility.
- A fixed income arbitrage strategy may include interest rate swap arbitrage, government bond arbitrage, forward yield curve arbitrage, and mortgage backed security arbitrage.
- An example of a fixed income arbitrage trade is when a hedge fund manager simultaneously buys an off-the-run Treasury bond and short sells an on-the-run Treasury bond.

#### Equity Market Neutral

- Equity market neutral strategy is designed to exploit equity martket inefficiencies and usually involves long and short matched equity portfolios of the same size.
- Well-designed equity market neutral portfolios typically control using a quantitative framework sector, style, market capitalization, and other exposures, which results in a beta neutral, near 50:50 balance of long and short positions.
- An example of a equity market neutral trade is one that is long US\$1 million of Company A share and short US\$1 million of Company B. This is to take advantage of under-valuation of company A and over-valuation of company B.

### Directional strategies

#### Equity long/short

- An equity long/short strategy involves holding both long and short equity positions with either a net long or net short exposures. The objective is not to be market neutral. Also, this category excludes long-only portfolios.
- Manangers with a long bias remain either net long or net short. Other managers may have a variable bias where they switch between net long and net short equity exposures.
- Some managers use a pair trading strategy and have a short position for each corresponding long position in a particular sector. Other managers take outright positions on both a long and short side, and only hedge dollar exposure and market exposure.

#### Global macro

- A global macro strategy involves opportunistically allocating captial among a wide variety of strategies and asset classes.
- Global macro is the most flexible hedge fund strategy, with managers often taking a top-down thematic approach to market opportunities.
- Suppose a global macro manager expects interest rate spreads between Japan and US to widen due to interest rate increases in Japan.
- The fund manager takes a position to buy the Japanses yen (and sell the US dollar) with the expectation that the Japanese yen will rise against the US dollar following an increase in Japanese interest rates.

#### Managed futures

- A managed futures strategy is bssed on speculating on the direction in market prices of currencies, commodities, equity indices, etc.
- Trading disciplines are generally systematic or discretoinary. Systematic managers use price and market specific information (often technical) to follow trends. Discretionary managers use a less quantitative approach, relying on both fundamental and technical analysis.
- Typical trades for a managed futures strategy include:
    - Trend following using technical analysis with a stop-loss
    - Long term directional trading based on fundamentals
    - Short term spot trading based on flow information

### Event-driven strategies

#### Merger arbitrage

- Merger arbitrage managers exploit merger activity to capture the spread between current market values and their values in the event of a merger.
- Managers generally consider a transaction once an announcement is publicly mande.
- Before entering into a trade, the manager analyzes the probability of the deal closing, the bid price, and the timeframe to the closing date. The probability of the takeover's success directly influences the size of the positions that the manager will take.
- The typical trade involves buying the stock of the target company (acquiree) and selling the stock of the acquiring company (acquirer). A key risk of this strategy is deal risk.

#### Distressed securities

- Distressed or high-yield securities are generally below investment grade and require expensive due diligence to take advantage of the low prices at which they trade.
- Distressed managers vary in terms of the capital structure in which they invest (debt or equity), the stage of the resutructuring process, and the degree to which they become involved in managing the restructuring.
- In certain cases, managers may attain a seat on the board and take an activist role, or even pursue litigation against the company or its directors.
- In analyzing distressed securities, the hedge fund considers the following questions: Does the business have long term value? Is the company having problems which can be rectified? Are the operating metrics declining? What class of debt have the highest priority in a restructuring?
