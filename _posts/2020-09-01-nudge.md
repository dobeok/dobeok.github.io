Interfaces can be consciously designed to nudge users into taking behaviors that benefit businesses.

- **Anchoring.** In an experiment, participants are asked to write down the last two digits of their social security number (essentially random). They are then asked to guess the price for a bottle of wine. Without further information, people who wrote larger numbers tend to guess higher prices. Businesses can abuse this behavour of customers for their benefit.
- **People tend not to change the default options**. In countries where organ donation is the default choice, the percentage of registered donor is higher than in countries where people opt-in.
- **Using descriptive norms as predictive rule**. Eg. 75% of people use less than 1 plastic bag per day. This can get people to change their behaviours.
- **Gamify.** Uber's nudge drivers to stay on the road longer by showing how far they are to the next earning tiers.
- **Psychological pricing.** Ending price values at "9", such as \$9.9 instead of \$10.0
- **Fake urgency.** Countdown clocks for sales creates a sense of urgency for buyers who are conlemplating purchase.
